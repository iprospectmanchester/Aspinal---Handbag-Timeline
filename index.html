The Evolution Of The Handbag

In celebration of National Handbag Day, Aspinal takes a walk through the history of the handbag. From its humble beginnings as a practical necessity, the handbag quickly became a marker of social standing, an expression of personal style and a fashion icon in its own right. Discover how the form and function of the handbag evolved over the centuries, and explore its significance within history, culture and style. Explore our timeline as we unpick how your favourite accessory came to be.

1300

The 14th Century

The handbag began life as a simple, functional leather pouch worn around the waist. These then progressed into ‘Hamondeys’, the ornate incarnation of the pouch that was worn to indicate high social standing.

1400

The 15th Century

Handbags earned sentimental value as pouches embroidered with love stories became a popular gift for a groom to bestow upon his bride. For everyday pursuits, durable ‘Chaneries’ were worn for more practical uses such as carrying food, hunting equipment and game.

1500

The 16th Century

As decadent fashion took centre stage, handbags returned to their practical roots. Women wore pouches under their skirts whilst men adopted ‘Bagges’; an early form of the pocket. The higher classes also wore ‘Swete Bagges’ filled with lavender to disguise bad odours, while peasants wore large cross-body bags out of a necessity to carry all their belongings with them.

1600

The 17th Century

Men’s clothes began to feature internal pockets, taking handbags solely into the female domain for the first time. Designs became much more ornate; however they were still worn underneath women’s skirts.

1700

The 18th Century

As neo-classicism introduced slim silhouettes, handbags were liberated from petticoats and new ‘Reticules’ were worn around the wrist. Crafted from fine fabrics to match a woman’s outfit, handbags were considered a fashion accessory for the first time.

1800

The 19th Century

The term ‘handbag’ came into use for the first time, describing the handheld luggage designed for railway travel. No longer worn around the wrist, women now suspended their ornate bags from chatelaines, broadening the horizons of handbag design.

1900

The 20th Century

Many innovations were made after the advent of luggage. Handbags were sturdier, featuring hinged frames, clasps and handles, and leather and crocodile skin were popular materials. The term ‘handbag’ came to encompass both men’s and women’s bags.

1910

The 1910's

As design became responsive to everyday life, functional shoulder bags and men’s briefcases were introduced. The ‘Pochette’, an early incarnation of the clutch bag, became popular among style conscious women.

1920

The 1920's

Pochettes adorned with geometric motifs became the bag of choice as the style rules were rewritten and it was no longer necessary for a woman to match her bag to her outfit. Novelty styles also made their debut and some particularly chic women chose to carry miniature dolls equipped with matching handbags.

1930

The 1930's

As Art Deco reigned, the modern clutch entered the mainstream, and it’s structured exquisitely designed cousin, the Van Cleefs & Arpels ‘minaudière’, became a favourite among Hollywood stars. The more functional satchel and shoulder bag came into being as plastic and zippers opened up new design possibilities.

1940

The 1940's

Gucci’s Bamboo Bag became the first ‘It Bag’. The iconic bamboo handles were born out of a need to innovate in the face of war time austerity. As women entered the workforce, simple large shoulder bags answered the day to day practicalities of carrying their belongings.

1950

The 1950's

For the first time, celebrity influence propelled a handbag into aspirational territory. The Hermès ‘Kelly’ bag assumed its place in handbag history in 1956. It was named after Grace Kelly following press photograph of her wearing the style to shield her pregnancy. Smaller handbags became favoured as a feminine counterpoint to functional styles of the previous decade, with the release of the Chanel 2.55 in 1955 popularising the concept of shoulder bags as a fashion accessory.

1960

The 1960's

Traditional structure gave way to slouchy leather after Gucci’s relaxed ‘Jackie’ leather shoulder bag, named after Jackie Onassis, gained popularity early in the decade.

As the 1960s wore on, the offbeat style of Mary Quant and Pucci encapsulated the new found freedom of style with their clashing, geometric design narratives. Luxury sales dropped as craftsmanship and quality were overlooked in favour of eccentric, inexpensive design.

1970

The 1970's

Young people’s travels to India influenced the popularity of large satchels and casual, fabric shoulder bags. Second wave feminism prompted women to project their sense of strength outwardly by wearing study handbags finished with multiple zips and heavy duty buckles. Meanwhile Loewe’s classic boxy ‘Amazona’ style crafted from unlined suede suggested a shift towards a more understated understanding of luxury design.

1980

The 1980's

Consumerism reigned and conspicuously branded handbags became a status symbol. The reissue of the Chanel 2.55 sparked a trend for quilting and chain handles, with Vera Bradley’s cotton quilted bags garnering $1million in sales in just three years. The Hermès ‘Birkin’ bag was also released in 1984, its roomy timeless design being the precursor to the 00s It Bag.

1990

The 1990's

The handbag’s social currency was evidenced by the rise in designer counterfeits. Leather remained the desirable material of choice, but denim, faux fur, mesh and plastic pushed the boundaries, with Longchamp’s nylon canvas ‘Le Pliage’ bridging the gap between luxury and affordability. The Fendi ‘Baguette’ marked the start of the It Bag era after rising to prominence through Sex and the City and selling over 100,000 units in its first year.

2000

The 2000's

The era of the It Bag hit full swing as handbags became the luxury investment purchase of choice for those on more modest incomes, with prices at the high-end of the market soaring as demand grew. Prada’s bowling bag sold out instantly, whilst the Chloè ‘Paddington’ sold all 8000 units created for spring 2005 setting a precedent for the appeal of limited run handbags.

Brands now recognised the influence of celebrities and socialites, with a single press photo having the power to sell out a particular style before it reached the shop floor. The Marc Jacobs ‘Stam’, named after model Jessica Stam, was so sought after that waiting lists had to be closed. ‘Man bags’ similarly grew in popularity. Born out of practicality, they paved the way for bags as men’s accessories.

2010

2010 - Future

The handbag landscape has opened up as neon, faux fur, charms and geometric structures take centre stage. The timeless leather shopper remains a reference point for premium labels, while men’s bags have soared in popularity. No longer a practicality, they are considered a stylish contemporary accessory.

The future of the handbag lies in integrated tech, with innovative new designs allowing you to match your bag to your outfit using colour-change abilities. Bags featuring built-in phone charges will also be prevalent, Ralph Lauren’s ‘Rickie’ bag already becoming the first to not only include a charging port but also LED lights which illuminate the interiors when the bag is opened.

Shapes and styles will be more varied than ever before but a reversion back to past decades will be seen as smaller delicate pouches and clutches taking centre stage once more.
